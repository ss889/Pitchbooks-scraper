AI is now a universal focus for retail banks, with generative AI use cases emerging across operations, from fraud prevention to customer service. As agentic AI systems gain traction, the bar is rising. Banks are no longer judged on experimentation alone, but on their readiness to deploy production-grade AI systems that can operate safely and at scale across the enterprise heading into 2026. To assess this shift, CB Insights‚Äô AI Readiness Index for Retail Banking ranks the top 20 retail banks in North America and Europe based on how actively they are building, partnering, and hiring to operationalize AI. The index goes beyond announcements and pilots to evaluate earnings transcripts, partnerships, investments, acquisitions, and patent filings. Notably, our analysis shows a widening gap . A small group of leaders has built the capabilities, infrastructure, and ecosystem leverage required to scale autonomous AI workflows repeatedly and safely. Others remain active but fragmented, with AI efforts that struggle to translate into bank-wide impact. We outline the key takeaways below. Key takeaways JPMorgan Chase is using AI spend to turn scale into a durable moat. Its coordinated AI strategy across internal builds, partnerships, and equity investments allows it to deploy and scale AI across the bank without fragmentation. This level of integration is difficult for peers with fewer resources or weaker operating models to replicate. Citigroup is narrowing the gap between AI investment and measurable value. By pairing proprietary tools with targeted investments, the bank is building the foundational infrastructure needed to support agentic AI experimentation and future scale. Internal builds are emerging as the control layer for large banks. Among top-tier institutions, AI readiness tracks closely with proprietary development. Bank of America and Wells Fargo are leaning into patents and internal builds to keep maximum control over governance as AI systems move closer to autonomy. Ecosystem partnerships offer a fast path to AI maturity. Lloyds Banking Group and BNP Paribas show how targeted alliances with LLM developers, point solutions, and infrastructure providers can close readiness gaps without matching the spend of global giants. LLM developer partnerships are becoming a key competitive lever for agentic AI. BNP Paribas and HSBC show how deep, cross-bank collaborations with LLM developers drive aggressive scale. By tapping Mistral AI ‚Äôs technology and ecosystem, the banks can ship use cases faster, moving to the front of the AI race. Signals from earnings transcripts, patent filings, and partnership data all point to the same inflection point: banks that bring agentic AI into production in 2026 will extend their lead, while those still confined to pilots will struggle as autonomous workflows become table stakes. JPMorgan Chase is turning AI spend into enterprise leverage. The agentic future favors industry heavyweights that can deploy at scale. JPMorgan Chase leads the AI Readiness Index, reflecting aggressive efforts across both execution and innovation. Since 2023, the bank has maintained activity across all AI readiness dimensions, from proprietary builds to external partnerships and equity investments. Jamie Dimon reinforced this focus in the Q1 2025 earnings call , signaling that AI isn‚Äôt a side project ‚Äì it‚Äôs central to JPMorgan Chase ‚Äôs competitive edge. ‚ÄúThe investment that we do in banks, branches, technology, AI is going to continue regardless of the environment.‚Äù This pattern extends to agentic AI. JPMorgan Chase has invested in governance- and workflow-focused startups such as FairPlay AI and Cresta , while continuing to integrate proprietary generative AI tools directly into internal workflows to support agent deployment . Rather than concentrating activity in a single area, the bank‚Äôs AI strategy is distributed across development, partnerships, and investments. This structure reduces reliance on any one vendor or technology path and supports repeatable deployment as AI systems become more autonomous. By contrast, lower-ranked banks tend to show concentrated activity in select areas without reinforcement across the stack, limiting their ability to scale agentic systems beyond pilot use cases. Citigroup connects AI investment activity to internal productivity gains The bank is using proprietary tools and strategic investments to build infrastructure for agentic AI initiatives. Ranked second in our AI Readiness Index based on overall activity, Citigroup is delivering measurable results from its AI initiatives, generating tangible internal ROI across coding, wealth management, and customer support. Its partnerships, which include RegTech firm Fenergo for agentic financial-crime prevention and Google Cloud for AI infrastructure modernization, reinforce this execution focus. Rather than experimenting in silos, Citigroup is integrating AI into platforms designed for broad employee adoption. Now, the bank is piloting an agentic upgrade to its workflow platform . Internal, enterprise initiatives like this one embody the cross-functional, integrated capabilities required to advance toward autonomous AI. Internal builds are becoming the control layer for large banks. Why proprietary AI will rule in the era of agentic AI Among the largest banks, AI readiness increasingly correlates with proprietary development. As AI systems move from assistants toward more autonomous agents, banks with extensive internal builds retain greater control over governance, integration, and risk management. Bank of America , ranked third, is the archetype. Despite having less than half JPMorgan Chase ‚Äôs market cap, it has filed more than twice as many AI-related patents since 2023. The filings reflect a strategy built on proprietary platforms and tools, including Erica, its generative AI virtual assistant; AskGPS, a generative AI assistant for business payments employees; and CashPro, a corporate transaction search tool. Wells Fargo is following a similar path. Since 2023, it has filed over 100 AI-related patents while developing employee-facing tools. Its 2025 partnership with Google Cloud Agentspace underscores the bank‚Äôs focus on building and controlling systems in house‚Äìnow for AI agents. Taken together, these signals suggest that for large banks, internal AI platforms are becoming a primary control layer as agentic systems move closer to production. While early AI adoption emphasized speed and experimentation, recent activity indicates a shift toward ownership and long-term operational control. Partnerships are a lever for AI readiness among smaller banks. How banks with smaller market caps are closing the AI readiness gap AI readiness does not require leadership across every dimension. Index results show that banks with smaller market caps can achieve higher readiness scores by relying more heavily on ecosystem partnerships. Lloyds Banking Group illustrates this pattern. Despite ranking 20th by market capitalization, the bank places 7th overal on the AI Readiness Index. Since 2023, Lloyds has concentrated its AI activity in partnerships, forming relationships with AI-focused companies including Unlikely AI , Cleareye.ai , and Google Cloud . With eight AI-focused business relationships, Lloyds Banking Group keeps pace with the top-ranked banks in the index. This approach allows the bank to access AI capabilities without matching the internal development or investment spend of larger institutions. At the same time, partnership-led strategies have limits as AI systems move toward greater autonomy. Lloyds‚Äô migration to Google Cloud ‚Äôs Vertex AI platform reflects a shift toward bringing more development in-house, complementing its external partnerships. This combination supports broader deployment while managing cost and organizational constraints. Deep, enterprise-level LLM partnerships accelerate AI readiness HSBC has thrown down a gauntlet to challenge first-mover BNP Paribas as both banks leverage Mistral AI for enterprise operations. BNP Paribas , fourth in the index, is scaling AI across the bank through its partnership with Mistral AI . Since 2023, the collaboration has expanded from Global Markets to multiple business lines, powering hundreds of use cases ranging from customer communications analysis to mortgage processing and ESG assessment. The alliance has delivered results on both sides. Since the partnership began, Mistral AI secured a $1.5B Series C and has seen a 12% increase in its Mosaic score, our proprietary ranking that measures a company‚Äôs success potential . These alliances will close agentic readiness gaps fast‚Äîbut only when embedded in core operations, not treated as isolated pilots.
