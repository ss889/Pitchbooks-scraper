The robotics sector raised a record $40.7B in 2025 ‚Äî up 74% YoY and 9% of all venture funding ‚Äî making it a funding leader alongside AI software. Physical AI is driving this progress, enabling robots to operate in the physical world and learn from data rather than pre-programmed rules. The race centers on building foundation models for robots to learn across tasks. Unlike language models trained on text, the physical AI models that power these systems require real-world robot data, creating an early window for market control. Model approaches: Core AI architectures that enable robots to understand their environment (VLMs), generate actions (VLAs), and predict outcomes (world models). Key takeaways Proprietary training data is a critical competitive advantage for physical AI, with tech leaders racing to secure access through acquisitions. Nvidia acquired synthetic data provider Gretel for over $320M in March 2025. Meta took a $14.8B stake in Scale , a data infrastructure and model development provider. OpenAI attempted to acquire Medal for its training data before Medal launched General Intuition to build models from that data. Companies controlling exclusive training data can develop more capable models while forcing competitors to license access. Securing diverse, high-quality training data early will determine which players achieve commercial scale. World models enable robots to predict and plan autonomously ‚Äî a capability beyond what large-language models (LLMs) can achieve. Investment in world models surged from $1.4B in 2024 to a record $6.9B in 2025, with companies in the space averaging a Mosaic score of 722 (the top 3% of all markets). But success requires quality training data from controlled environments and close partnerships with hardware manufacturers to ensure seamless performance in complex real-world tasks. Multi-robot coordination remains the critical missing piece in physical AI. Despite the two global foundation model leaders racing ahead with single-robot approaches ‚Äî US companies raised over $17B across 17 deals while Chinese companies raised $416M across 15 deals ‚Äî both focus almost exclusively on individual robot capabilities. Few companies are building the orchestration layer needed to manage multiple robot types working together. Robots need massive amounts of training data, but collecting real-world data is slow and expensive. These markets include synthetic data, real-world demonstrations, and simulations that allow robots to fail safely. Synthetic training data ‚Äî robotics : Tools that generate synthetic datasets ‚Äî images, sensor data, 3D scenes ‚Äî without expensive real-world collection. Robot demonstration data providers : Companies that capture real-world data ‚Äî teleoperation data, motion trajectories, and video ‚Äî for imitation learning. Robotics simulation platforms : Virtual environments where companies train, test, and validate robots before physical deployment. Data scarcity is the main bottleneck for physical AI, as real-world training data is difficult to access due to cost and availability. These markets average a Mosaic score of around 600 (placing them in the top half of market momentum), with 50% of companies now in the deployment stage , indicating that data infrastructure for physical AI is maturing beyond research into commercial viability. Many companies now rely on synthetic data and simulation , with Nvidia leading the market . However, synthetic data alone is not enough ‚Äî real-world robot data remains critical for reliable model training. Leaders here include Scale, which raised $16.4B and ranks in the top 1% of companies by Mosaic score. Scale combines both synthetic data generation and real-world data collection ‚Äî including human teleoperation of robots and sensor data from physical environments ‚Äî to extend its data labeling business into model development. Emerging players are pursuing new data sources to overcome existing shortages. For example, General Intuition raised $134M to train models on gameplay videos applicable to robotic systems, while micro1 raised $35M at a $500M valuation, building the largest global robotics training dataset from human interaction videos. Without proprietary datasets or simulation platforms, robot companies must license from these leaders or risk falling behind on critical data types like touch, pressure, and physical movement. MODEL APPROACHES These models give robots vision, reasoning, and the ability to act, each building on the others. Together, they enable robots to adapt to new tasks rather than follow rigid programming. Vision language model (VLM) developers : Multimodal models that combine visual understanding with natural language, serving as the perception layer for robots. Vision-language-action (VLA) model developers : Models that build off of VLMs by combining visual perception, language understanding, and motor control into an AI system that translates commands directly into physical actions. World model AI developers : Models that simulate how environments change to predict spatial relationships, physics, and cause-and-effect. Physical AI is shifting robotics from hard-coded programming to flexible, task-adaptive systems, with vision-language-action models emerging as a leading architecture. Leading humanoid robot companies ‚Äî such as Figure , 1X , and Galbot ‚Äî are building proprietary VLA models while big tech companies like Nvidia and Meta are developing models for commercial licensing to robot manufacturers. World models are possibly the real unlock by adding predictive reasoning to this stack, which is why companies like World Labs and Runway are building them. Unlike VLAs, which respond to immediate inputs, world models simulate how environments change over time, letting robots anticipate consequences, plan multi-step actions, and recover from errors. FOUNDATION MODELS Foundation models combine data and architecture into pre-trained robot intelligence that can perceive, reason, and act. Some are general-purpose for manipulation, while others are specialized. Developers can now license and adapt this intelligence rather than build from scratch. Robot foundation model developers : General-purpose models that help robots see, think, and move across different hardware. Autonomous driving foundation model developers : Models trained on large driving datasets that unify perception, prediction, planning, and control, in turn replacing traditional autonomous systems for robotaxis, trucking, and delivery. Multi-robot coordination model developers : Multi-agent algorithms that let robot swarms work together by handling task distribution, collision avoidance, and decentralized decision-making. The foundation models market remains highly dynamic. US tech giants ( Microsoft , Google , Amazon ) and Chinese leaders ( Huawei , Baidu ) compete alongside high-profile startups like DeepSeek and Physical Intelligence . Many companies work across multiple types of foundation models. Training data from one domain can improve model performance in others. Nvidia is the only company working across all three foundation model categories, positioning itself as infrastructure for the entire physical AI stack. Multi-robot coordination ‚Äî like humanoids working with autonomous mobile robots and autonomous forklifts in a warehouse ‚Äî is the next frontier. Coordinating multiple robot types requires an orchestration layer to manage tasks, resources, and collision avoidance without central control. Only a few startups ( Field AI and Intrinsic ) and big tech companies are pursuing it. Most work remains in research rather than commercial deployment. Companies with proprietary models may capture higher margins through differentiation and vertical integration. Those licensing third-party AI may benefit from lower costs as models commoditize, but compete on deployment speed and integration quality rather than core technology. OBSERVABILITY Observability captures what happens when robots operate in production, bridging the gap between lab development and real-world deployment. Robotics observability platforms : Platforms for monitoring, debugging, and optimizing robots in development and production. When robots fail or behave unexpectedly, engineers replay incidents, identify causes, and push fixes. Real-world edge cases feed into simulation and training, refining models and improving performance. Foxglove and Formant , for example, track performance, analyze failures, and feed deployment insights back into training datasets, simulations, and models. Companies with strong observability can rapidly improve their models by learning from failures. Unexpected situations are turned into training advantages, separating production-ready systems from lab prototypes.
